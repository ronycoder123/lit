Language and Literacy

Literacy

Karnataka’s literacy rate has steadily increased over time. In 1991, the literacy rate was around 50%, showing that only half of the population was literate at that time. By 2001, it rose to about 60%, indicating improvement in access to education. In 2011, the literacy rate further increased to nearly 70%, reflecting the impact of government education schemes, better schooling facilities, and increased awareness. The growth continued in the following years, reaching around 80% in 2021. By 2025, the literacy rate is shown to be approximately 90%, which indicates significant progress in education across the state. Overall, the graph clearly shows a consistent upward trend in Karnataka’s literacy rate, highlighting continuous development in the education sector and improved educational opportunities for the population.

Languages Spoken



The pie chart representing the Karnataka State Language Census (2011) shows a clear dominance of Kannada, which forms about 67% of the total population, establishing it as the linguistic backbone of the state. This strong majority acts as the base flavour, giving Karnataka its cultural identity. Urdu, at 11%, adds a distinct layer, followed by Telugu (6%) and Tamil (4%), reflecting historical migration and regional interaction. Smaller linguistic groups such as Marathi (3%), Tulu (3%), Lambani (2%), Hindi (1%), Konkani (1%), Malayalam (1%), and other languages (1%) contribute subtle but important variety, much like carefully balanced spices. Together, the chart highlights Karnataka’s rich linguistic diversity, where a dominant language coexists with several minority languages, creating a well-blended cultural composition.




Ladakh, a high-altitude region in northern India, is home to a rich tapestry of languages reflecting its diverse culture. The primary languages spoken here are Ladakhi, Balti, and Purgi, all of which belong to the Tibetan-Burmese family. Ladakhi is widely used in Leh and surrounding areas, serving as the mother tongue for most locals, while Balti is common in the Kargil region. Besides these, Shina and Hindi are also spoken by some communities, and English is used for administration and education. The multilingual nature of Ladakh not only helps preserve its ancient cultural traditions but also fosters communication across different ethnic groups, making the region linguistically vibrant and unique.
